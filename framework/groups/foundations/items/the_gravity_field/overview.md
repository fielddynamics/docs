# The Gravity Field

Gravity is a field. Not a force that reaches across empty space. Not a mysterious pull between distant objects. It lattices spacetime, carries its own topology, and propagates. Every other field in physics, the electromagnetic field, the strong nuclear field, and the weak field, lives on a stage. These fields inherit their geometry from a background spacetime that already exists. Gravity is different. Gravity is the stage. It does not borrow structure from an external background the way other fields do. Its structure is not imposed from outside. It arises from the topology of the fields that compose it, and that topology, as we will see, is shared with the field that carries light itself.

In September 2015, the LIGO observatory recorded this directly. Two black holes, spiralling into each other over a billion light years away, produced a ripple in the gravitational field that travelled across the universe at the speed of light and arrived at Earth as a gravitational wave.

Two years later, a neutron star collision confirmed that these waves travel at exactly the speed of light. The gravitational field carries waves. It propagates.

Now consider what propagation requires. An ocean is not one enormous water molecule. It is a topology of water molecules, and it is precisely that topology, molecule coupled to molecule in a connected lattice, that allows a wave to cross it. No single molecule travels from one shore to the other. Each molecule pushes its neighbour, that neighbour pushes the next, and the wave passes forward through a chain of local interactions. The wave exists because the water has parts, and those parts are connected.

Gravitational waves work the same way. Each region of the gravitational field influences its immediate neighbours through the geometric structure that general relativity requires. The wave passes from region to region, at the speed of light, across billions of light years.

This is not a metaphor. The wave equation that governs gravitational propagation contains a mathematical operation called the Laplacian, and the Laplacian, by its definition, computes the difference between a point and its surrounding neighbours. It cannot be evaluated in isolation. It requires knowledge of what is happening next door.

LIGO detected gravitational waves. Therefore gravity has neighbours. Therefore the gravitational field has parts. Therefore it has structure.

The gravitational field is not a monolithic, undifferentiated whole. It is a topology: many locally coupled field regions, each one interacting with the ones adjacent to it, forming a connected architecture that spans all of spacetime.

The parts have geometry. And the question that this framework is built to answer is immediate: if the gravitational field carries waves, and waves require structure, what is the structure of that field?
The answer begins with counting. In three spatial dimensions, the minimum number of faces required to enclose a volume is four. That number determines everything that follows.
