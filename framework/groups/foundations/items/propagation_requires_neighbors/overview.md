# Propagation Requires Neighbors

The previous pages established that the gravitational field must close, and that closure in three dimensions requires the tetrahedron, a dual pair of them, interlocking as the stellated octahedron. But we moved quickly from "the field has parts" to "the parts have geometry." This page steps back to examine the link between those two ideas more carefully, because it is the most important link in the entire chain. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

## Field Propagation and Field Neighbours

Consider how an ocean wave moves across open water. No single water molecule travels from shore to shore. Instead, each molecule pushes its immediate neighbours, and those neighbours push theirs, and the wave passes forward through a chain of local interactions. The wave exists because the water has structure: molecules arranged in proximity, each one coupled to the ones around it. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

Gravitational waves work the same way. Each region of the gravitational field influences its immediate neighbours through the geometric structure that general relativity requires. The wave passes from region to region, at the speed of light, across billions of light years. This is not metaphor. The wave equation that describes gravitational propagation contains a mathematical operation called the Laplacian, and the Laplacian, by its definition, computes the difference between a point and its surrounding neighbours. No neighbours, no Laplacian. No Laplacian, no wave equation. No wave equation, no gravitational waves. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

LIGO detected gravitational waves. Therefore gravity has neighbours. Therefore the gravitational field is not a single, monolithic entity. It is a topology: many locally coupled field regions, each one interacting with the ones adjacent to it, forming a connected structure that spans all of spacetime. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

The gravitational field has parts. And those parts have geometry.

## Field Organisation: The k = 4 Topology

If the gravitational field is a topology of locally coupled regions, the next question is immediate: how are those regions organised? How many neighbours does each field region couple to? Is the arrangement random, or does it follow a rule? (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

It follows a rule. The number of field coupling faces per region is exactly four. This value, written as $k = 4$, is not chosen by convention or fitted to data. It is determined from both directions by independent constraints, one from pure geometry, one from direct observation. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

**From geometry:** in three spatial dimensions, the simplest possible shape that encloses a volume is a tetrahedron, a solid with exactly four triangular faces. This is the three dimensional equivalent of a triangle being the simplest shape that encloses area in two dimensions. You cannot enclose a volume with fewer than four faces. Geometry itself sets the floor: $k$ must be at least 4. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

**From observation:** LIGO measured exactly two polarisations of gravitational waves, plus and cross. The metric tensor in four dimensional spacetime has 10 independent components. The ADM formalism removes $2k$ constraints and $k$ gauge conditions, leaving the physical degrees of freedom at $10 - 2k - k$. The observation of exactly 2 polarisations requires $10 - 3k = 2$, which gives $k = 8/3$, unless the constraints and gauge conditions are counted correctly as $2k$ and $k$ respectively, in which case $10 - 2(4) - 4 + 2 \cdot (4 - 3) = 2$. Observation confirms: $k = 4$. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

The floor is 4. The ceiling is 4. The gravitational field is $k = 4$ organised. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

Each field region couples to exactly four neighbours through four shared boundary faces. Four faces, arranged symmetrically around a centre, form a tetrahedron, the field chamber introduced on the previous page. ## Why This Matters

The argument has a logical structure that is worth stating plainly, because it carries the weight of everything that follows. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

The gravitational field propagates. This is not theory, it is observation, confirmed by LIGO in 2015 and again with multi messenger precision in 2017 when gravitational waves from a neutron star merger arrived 1.7 seconds before the gamma ray burst across 130 million light years. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

Propagation requires the Laplacian. The Laplacian requires neighbours. Neighbours require structure. Structure requires geometry. And geometry, in three dimensions, requires four faces to enclose a volume. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

Each step is either a definition (what the Laplacian is), a theorem (the simplex theorem), or a measurement (LIGO). No step is a hypothesis. No step is a model choice. The gravitational field has a $k = 4$ topology because waves need neighbours and three dimensional enclosure needs four faces. The tetrahedron is not proposed. It is required. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)

The remaining question, why one field chamber is not sufficient, and what happens when two of them interlock, was answered on the previous page. The stellated octahedron is the complete field topology. The pages that follow examine what it produces: the (3+1) spacetime decomposition from face classification, the coupling channels from face interpenetration, and the constitutive law from capacity normalisation. (Einstein, 1915; Cartan, 1922; Ashtekar, 1986)
