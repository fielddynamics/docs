# Dual Tetrahedron and Field Origin

The previous page established that field closure in three dimensions requires four faces, and that four faces form a tetrahedron, the simplest solid enclosing volume. This is a single field chamber: the minimal local unit of the gravitational field.
A single field chamber is not enough.

Think of a single tetrahedron sitting in space. It has four faces, four vertices, and it encloses a volume. But it also has an orientation: its apex points in a definite direction. Flip it upside down, and you get a different tetrahedron pointing the other way. A gravitational field that only knew about one orientation would be incomplete. It would distinguish between "up" and "down" at the most fundamental level, and gravity does not do this. The gravitational field couples to mass regardless of orientation. It must be symmetric under spatial inversion.

This is not a new requirement. In tetrad gravity, the local frame must support both chiralities of matter (left handed and right handed fermions). In bimetric gravity, Hassan and Rosen showed that a consistent theory requires two interacting tetrads. In loop quantum gravity, Ashtekar's variables decompose the gravitational field into self dual and anti self dual sectors. (Ashtekar, 1986) Each formalism arrives at the same conclusion through different reasoning: the gravitational field requires two field chambers, not one.

| Formalism | What requires two chambers | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Tetrad gravity | Fermion coupling requires both chiralities | Einstein (1928); Cartan (1922); Weyl (1929) |
| Bimetric gravity | Ghost-freedom requires two interacting tetrads | Hassan and Rosen (2012) |
| Loop Quantum Gravity | Ashtekar variables split into self dual and anti self dual | Ashtekar (1986) |
| Spinor theory | SL(2,C) double cover of the Lorentz group | Penrose and Rindler (1984) |
| Stability analysis | Single chamber field has a ghost mode (negative energy) | Boulware and Deser (1972) |

Five independent arguments. One conclusion. The gravitational field has two chambers.
## The Parity Partition

In this framework, the second field chamber is not postulated. It emerges from binary arithmetic.

The uniqueness equation gave us a cube with 8 vertices. Each vertex has a 3 bit binary address, 000 through 111, encoding its position along three spatial axes. Now count the number of active bits in each address. Vertices with an odd count (1, 2, 4, 7) fall into one group. Vertices with an even count (0, 3, 5, 6) fall into the other. This is the parity partition, and it is forced by the binary encoding itself.

Within each group, every pair of vertices differs in exactly two out of three bit positions. In Cartesian coordinates, this means the four vertices of each group are equally spaced from one another. Each group forms a perfectly regular tetrahedron. The two tetrahedra are vertex disjoint (they share no corners), edge disjoint (they share no edges), and face disjoint (they share no walls). They interlock inside the cube like two hands with interlaced fingers, each one's vertices penetrating the other's faces. The resulting structure is the stellated octahedron.

This geometry was not designed. It was inherited from binary arithmetic in three dimensions. The same equation that fixed d = 3 and k = 4 produces, automatically, a dual field chamber with the complete inversion symmetry that gravity requires.

## The Field Origin

Every vertex of the cube has a partner diagonally opposite: 000 pairs with 111, 001 with 110, 010 with 101, 011 with 100. These four body diagonals all pass through the exact centre of the cube, the single point equidistant from every vertex, where the position vectors of all eight corners cancel to zero.
This point is the Field Origin. It is the unique location of maximal symmetry in the structure, the place where the two field chambers meet face to face and couple. It is not a mathematical convenience. It is a geometric necessity: the only point where the dual field chambers can exchange information without breaking the inversion symmetry that defines them.
At the Field Origin, both field chambers are present simultaneously. Neither dominates. The field is balanced, symmetric, and fully coupled. Moving away from the Field Origin in any direction breaks this balance, one chamber begins to dominate over the other, and the gravitational field acquires a definite character. This is the mechanism by which the field transitions from one regime to another, and it is governed by the coupling structure of the faces where the two chambers interpenetrate.
## The Structural Inventory

Every structural constant of the stellated octahedron reduces to the two integers K = 4 and D = 3:

| Quantity | Expression | Value |
|---|---|---|
| Faces per tetrahedron | K | 4 |
| Vertices (total) | 2^D | 8 |
| Edges (total) | K x D | 12 |
| Structural elements (edges + origin) | K x D + 1 | 13 |
| Coupling channels (face x face) | K^2 | 16 |

Twelve edges, all of uniform length. One shared origin. Thirteen structural elements in total. The number 13 reappears throughout the framework as the coupling denominator, the geometric floor of the field's capacity. The number 16, four faces coupling through four sub channels each, determines the strength of the gravitational interaction between the two chambers.

The stellated octahedron is the complete topology of the gravitational field.
